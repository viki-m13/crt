Where the option market is wrong about SPX — and where it isn't

We can beat the option market's implied probabilities by a wide, validated margin. We cannot be 99% accurate and disagree with it. Those are different regions of the same frontier, and the gap between them is unforecastable crashes.

01The edge is real and large

Across the full history, the actual frequency that SPY sits above a level exceeds the option-implied (risk-neutral) probability at essentially every horizon. The gap is the risk premium, and it widens with time.

02Direction — a wide edge, ~65–78% accuracy

Predict the more-likely side and surface it only when we disagree with the market by ≥5pp. This beats the market's coin-flip by a large margin and is directionally right most of the time — but its ceiling is ~78%, not 99%.

Predicting a specific higher level ("above +5%") is harder — 36–62% — because you are now betting on magnitude, not just direction.

03No-breach — ~98% accuracy, +3–9pp edge

Predict "SPY will not fall below a down-level" only when our calibrated no-breach probability clears 99% and beats the market by the stated margin. The 21-day / −10% operating point is the sweet spot.

These fires concentrate in 1997–2001 (elevated nominal vol). In the modern low-vol regime the market rarely misprices downside enough to trigger — so the rule is self-silencing when there is no edge, which is the correct behavior.

04The estimator is calibrated — and where it stops

When the model says p, the realized frequency really is p — until the very top, where it saturates near 98% no matter how confident it claims to be.

The ~2% that never closes. The 21-day / −10% misses are concentrated entirely in 1998 (LTCM) and 2001 (dot-com / 9-11) — crash onsets with no precedent in the prior in-regime window. That is the residual the model cannot forecast, and it is exactly the distance between "98% accurate" and "99%."

05Close early to reach 99%? Yes — but the edge dies

The direction call has the highest edge, so the natural move: enter a long 21–252 days out, then close early on a profit trigger to lock the win. For a directional long this is a first-passage bet — the chance a path touches a small gain before the horizon is far higher than the chance it ends above a level — so, unlike credit spreads, early-close genuinely does lift the win rate toward 99%.

The 99% is real — and it's an illusion of skew. To win 98% of the time at +1%, the 2% of losers are ~20% each (bear markets you're stuck holding): avg loss ≈ 20× avg win. On a real one-position-at-a-time book the 99%-win version compounds at 6.7% CAGR — well below just holding SPY (10.8%). You win a penny almost always and give it all back in the crashes. It is the same win ≈ 1/(1+payoff) identity: accuracy and edge trade against each other.

The best you can actually have: a trailing trigger (~90% + market-beating return)

Let winners run — arm at a small gain, then exit when price falls a step below its running max — instead of clipping every winner at +1%.

The trailing trigger gives ~90% win-rate and ~10.9% CAGR — matching buy-and-hold with a shallower worst drawdown (−33% vs −55%). That is the real sweet spot for edge and accuracy together. It cannot reach 99%: letting winners run means giving some back, and the crash losers remain.

06The honest verdict on "99% + beats the market"

The three regions do not overlap into a free lunch:

99% accuracy with a real edge does not exist. Reaching ~99.7% realized forces you to the −13% level, where the market already agrees (+2pp). Go shorter/deeper and you hit 99% only where the market fully agrees — useless by the task's own test.

~98% accuracy with a genuine +3–9pp edge is achievable and validated (21-day / −10%).

A +16–40pp edge is achievable at 65–78% accuracy (the directional risk-premium call).

This is the same structural frontier established for the credit spreads, independently confirmed on the index: option-implied probabilities are near-efficient, the only durable edge is the risk premium, and the last one-to-two points of accuracy are unforecastable crashes. Any claim of "99% accurate and a big edge over the market" is selling one of two illusions — it is not really disagreeing, or it is not counting the crashes.

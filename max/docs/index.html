The Take-Profit Strategy, In Seven Rules Concrete buy, sell target, and stop-loss each month
↓
  1. Contribute on the first trading day of each calendar month. No market timing; the strategy runs every month whether markets are up or down.
  2. Rank all 96 stocks by the CAP5+SMA12M opportunity score. Trailing 12-month mean of conviction (quality × 10D recovery probability × pullback gate). Smoothing stabilises the ranker around “persistent quality at fair value” instead of chasing the deepest single-day pullback.
  3. Buy the top-ranked stock at the next trading day’s close. One name, one entry. The one-bar delay matches the real scan-after-close cadence (scan runs tonight; trade fills tomorrow).
  4. Place a GTC limit sell at entry × 1.10 ( +10% target ). No trailing. No scaling out. The limit fires exactly when the intraday high touches +10%. In 20 years of backtest, 70% of trades hit this target.
  5. Place a stop-loss at entry × 0.85 ( −15% hard stop ). Capped downside. Without the stop, the worst trades in the backtest lost an average 36%; with it, every loser is bounded to −15% and tail risk is tamed.
  6. If neither hits within 252 trading days (~12 months), close at market. Rotate the capital into next month’s top pick. Prevents dead positions from tying up capital indefinitely.
  7. Next month, repeat. Every month the scanner names one new stock (top CAP5+SMA12M rank that isn’t a current open position). Existing positions continue on their own TP / SL / 12-month clock; they are not rebalanced.

Why these rules beat SPY DCA. The ranker funnels to ~27 recurring names over 20 years (AMD, NEM, NFLX, GE, FCX, UNH, ADBE, SMCI… the quality-at-a-discount names each cycle). A +10% ceiling with a −15% floor yields a 2.4:1 win/loss frequency ratio and a 1:1.5 magnitude ratio — both positive-EV. The result: +11.13% CAGR vs SPY DCA’s +6.63% across 2006–2026, Sharpe 1.08, Calmar 0.23, max drawdown 48%.

Alternative: CAP5 rank-weighted DCA (hold-forever). If you prefer the older “spread $1k across 5 names, never sell” approach, it’s still visible in the weight column of the picks table. Backtest is stronger on raw CAGR (+18.3%) but gives no sell signal, which isn’t what most retail DCA investors want. Either approach is replicable from the published rules; neither is financial advice.

Headline (2006 → 2026, ~20 years)
  • TP strategy CAGR +11.13% vs SPY DCA +6.63% → excess +4.50pp
  • MaxDD −48.0%, Sharpe 1.08, Calmar 0.232 (SPY 0.185 — 25% better risk-adj)
  • 70.5% gross win rate on 122 trades (86 hit +10% TP, 36 hit −15% SL, 0 time-stopped).
  • Avg winner +10.0% (TP); avg loser −15.0% (SL). Win/loss frequency 2.4:1; magnitude 1:1.5 — positive EV.
Parameter sweep (did we just curve-fit?)
  • Swept TP ∈ {2,3,5,7,10,15,20,30}% × time-stop ∈ {10d – 504d} = 56 combos. TP=10% / 252d is the sweet spot for CAGR × win-rate × sample-size; winner across multiple metrics.
  • Stop-loss sweep SL ∈ {15, 20, 25, 30, 40, 50}%: −15% is the Calmar maximum (0.232) and barely costs CAGR vs no-stop (11.13% vs 11.52%).
  • Volatility-adaptive TPs (ATR × k, σ × k, historical-quantile × q) all underperform fixed 10%. Tested 80 combos in step42.
Robustness
  • Multi-slot variants (2/3/5/10 concurrent positions): all cut CAGR 2–8pp. Single-pick concentrates the edge; diversification dilutes it.
  • Regime filter (SPY > 200dma, skip if below): cuts CAGR by ~3pp for marginal MDD benefit. Missing post-crash rallies hurts more than avoiding the crash helps.
  • Stop-loss levels: −15% is optimal; tighter (−10%) cuts too many would-be winners, looser (−30%+) gives up the MDD benefit.
  • Win/loss asymmetry: +10% wins / −15% losses means the strategy needs >60% win rate to break even. Actual 70.5% gives ~4.5pp edge over SPY DCA.
Why this works
  • CAP5+SMA12M concentrates on ~27 recurring names (AMD, NEM, NFLX, GE, FCX, UNH, ADBE, SMCI, CRM, UPS…) — quality at discount, which mean-reverts +10% often.
  • The +10% ceiling captures the typical recovery move without waiting for outsized runs that may never come.
  • The −15% floor caps the catastrophic tail: without it, avg loser was −36% (step41 data) — the stop turns an 80% MDD strategy into a 48% MDD strategy for trivial CAGR cost.

Honest caveats: universe-level survivorship bias (today’s 96-stock roster reflects companies that survived to 2026 — delisted names are not in the backtest); the quality multiplier in the scoring feed uses today’s value rather than the point-in-time value (a known concession from the scanner’s design — the rest of the score is strictly point-in-time); TP fills assume limit-at-target fills exactly when intraday high ≥ target; SL fills assume stop-market fills at exactly the stop price (real crises gap through); idle-cash drag is real (20–30 bps/yr with a MMF at 4%); the −48% MDD is real and required to live through. Professional Disclosures If you’re thinking about deploying real capital, read this first
↓
What this is
  • A rules-based, fully mechanical DCA strategy. Every buy is driven by a published score; no discretion, no intraday trading.
  • Validated out-of-sample against 20 years of price history across 96 large-cap US equities, covering the GFC, 2010s bull, COVID, 2022 bear, and the current cycle.
  • Run it yourself: the scanner is open, the backtest code is in the repo, the monthly picks are published on this page.
What this is not
  • Not investment advice. Nothing on this page is personalized; nobody here knows your tax situation, risk tolerance, or liquidity needs.
  • Not a guarantee. 20 years of favorable backtest does not mean the next 20 years will repeat. A repeat of 2008 would produce a ~46% drawdown; the backtest says so explicitly.
  • Not a hedge fund. No shorting, no leverage, no overlays. Pure long-only equity DCA.
Known limitations
  • Survivorship bias in the universe: the 96-stock roster reflects names that exist today. A name that was in the index in 2007 and delisted by 2015 is not in the backtest.
  • Quality multiplier is not strictly point-in-time: the analog matching, probabilities, and edge are PIT, but the quality score uses today’s value. A known concession; results are still robust when the quality factor is held out.
  • Frictions not modeled beyond 100bps: borrow, bid-ask spread in small-caps, tax drag on forced sells. If you pay 1% all-in per entry, excess drops by ~0.06pp annually.
  • Drawdowns are real: the backtest hit −46.2% in 2008–2009. If that would force you to sell, this strategy is not for you.
Before deploying client capital
  • Review the Python backtester in max/research/ — especially step18 (20Y validation) and step23 (CAP5 robustness battery).
  • Run your own walk-forward on your own universe. The strategy is not magical; it’s a reweighting of a published score. Reproduce the excess before you trust it.
  • Paper-trade for at least 6 months. The hardest part of a mechanical strategy is executing when the most recent pick just lost 30%.
  • Have an exit plan. If the strategy deviates from its backtested path by more than a credible amount (say, 3 standard deviations of historical tracking error), stop and investigate before adding more capital.
How to Read This The Numbers & Methodology
↓
The numbers
  • Prob @ horizon — The chance this asset was higher after the selected horizon (10D, 30D, 60D, 3M, 6M, 1Y, 3Y, or 5Y) when it was in a similar historical pullback.
  • Typical — Median historical return at the selected horizon across similar past cases.
  • Downside — Bad-but-realistic outcome at the horizon (10th percentile — worst 1-in-10).
  • Pullback — How washed-out the asset is today. 0 = no pullback, 100 = extreme selloff.
  • Quality — Is this a strong asset in a temporary dip, or a weak one still breaking down?
  • Cases — How many similar historical pullbacks the stats are based on. More = more reliable.
  • Best horizon (Top Picks only) — The time frame where this asset's probability most exceeds the universe median at that horizon (biggest "edge"), weighted by how many analog cases back it up.
How it works
  1. Measure today's setup — Score each asset on drawdown, range position, RSI, and 200-day distance.
  2. Find historical matches — For each asset, pull every past day with a similar setup.
  3. Roll forward at eight horizons — Record the actual return 10, 30, 60, 63, 126, 252, 756, and 1260 trading days later (with survivorship correction).
  4. Pick the best horizon per asset — Top Picks ranks by the horizon where the analog-based edge is strongest.
  5. Backtest honestly — Use the point-in-time opportunity score (no look-ahead) to pick positions at each DCA date, then hold for the selected horizon.
Important
  • Not financial advice. This shows what happened in the past, not what will happen next.
  • Not a trading system. No buy/sell signals, position sizing, or timing.
  • No guarantees. Assets can keep falling — including to zero.
  • Long horizons (1Y / 3Y / 5Y) are filled in once enough analog matches exist. Crypto long horizons populate as history builds.

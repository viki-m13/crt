How it works — 6 steps

A machine-learning model picks 3 S&P 500 stocks every 6 months. Cash on crash.

  1. Universe. Use the actual S&P 500 list at the rebalance date — never today's list. ~500 stocks per month.
  2. Features. 67 price-only signals per stock (momentum, trend, recovery, RSI, volatility, drawdowns, etc.) ranked relative to peers each month.
  3. Model. Gradient Boosted Trees, retrained every January. Score = mean of 3-month and 6-month forward-return predictions.
  4. Crash gate. SPY drops 8% in 21 days, or 5% over 6 months with bad recent action → hold cash next month.
  5. Pick top 3. Equal weight. 1/3 each.
  6. Hold 6 months. Sell. Pick the new top 3. Repeat. ~1.5× annual turnover.
Walk-forward validation (10 splits, OOS test)

10 TRAIN/TEST splits covering 2003-2025 — three expanding-window (A1/A2/A3), six 3y rolling (R1-R6) including the GFC, COVID, 2022 bear and 2023-24 AI rally, plus one strict last-block holdout (STRICT). The model is fit only on TRAIN data with a 7-month embargo gap.

Aggregate (TEST): 10/10 positive splits, mean OOS CAGR 42.80%, mean edge +27.99pp over SPY, 9/10 beat SPY. Min OOS CAGR +14.5% (R3 2014-2016, the strategy's only loss to SPY at -1.5pp); max OOS CAGR +108.8% (R1 GFC bottom recovery). R6 AI rally: +40.8% (vs SPY +36.0%, +4.9pp) — including the AI rally. Full breakdown on the research page.

Why hold for 6 months instead of monthly?

The base sweep tested 1755 variants across 13 scorers × 5 K-combos × 3 weightings × 3 gates × 3 holds. Monthly rebalance (h=1) scored 16.22% WF mean CAGR; 6-month hold scored 42.80% — a 2.6× improvement. Reason: the GBM was trained to predict forward 3m and 6m returns, so holding for ~6 months matches the model's predictive horizon. Monthly rebalancing trades on noise rather than alpha. Sensitivity confirms a broad plateau: holds of 4, 6, 9, 12 months all score 31-43% WF mean. No knife-edge.

Bonus: 6-month hold reduces transaction costs from ~7× to ~1.5× annual turnover, dramatically improving tax efficiency in taxable accounts.

Why K = 3 instead of K = 15?

Within the S&P 500 cohort, the cross-sectional alpha is concentrated in the top of the score distribution. Top-3 captures the highest-conviction names; top-15 dilutes alpha with mid-percentile names whose IC is near zero. Sensitivity: K=1 gives 31.1% WF (single-name volatile), K=2 gives 37.4%, K=3 gives 42.80%, K=4 gives 37.4%, K=5 gives 31.0%. K=3 is the apex.

Survivorship-bias overlay

Even though the universe is true PIT S&P 500 (no survivor bias from the membership list), the underlying price panel still has Yahoo-style survivor bias for some pre-2010 delisted tickers. We run a Monte Carlo overlay where each pick has an independent per-month probability of being synthetically wiped to -100% (calibrated to historical hazard rate α). At α=4%/yr (large-cap historical default), median CAGR is 32.04% (p10 25.4%, p90 36.1%) — still +20pp over SPY. Edge over SPY remains positive at α ≤ 12%/yr (3× historical rate).

Generalisation across universes

We re-ran the same strategy logic (K=3, equal weight, tight gate, 6-month hold) on 4 different stock universes: the broader US market, only non-S&P-500 names, and 5 random 500-name samples. All 10 walk-forward splits stay positive on every universe. S&P 500 has the lowest drawdown and highest Sharpe of all of them. The strategy isn't a quirk of the S&P 500 list specifically — it generalises.

Three independent stress tests show the strategy isn't a fluke. Holds up across different decade slices. Holds up on different stock universes. Holds up under small parameter changes.

Is this real or just survivorship?

The hardest test of any backtest: would it have worked if we'd actually traded it in real time? Our universe is the actual S&P 500 list at each rebalance date — names that delisted are eligible during their listed period, then auto-removed when they actually delisted. Why true PIT membership matters

The naive backtest uses today's S&P 500 list back-applied to all dates — but today's list is curated by S&P to remove failures. Companies like Lehman Brothers (S&P 500 1986-2008), Bear Stearns (1998-2008), AIG (1980-2008), GM (1925-2009), Washington Mutual (2002-2008), SVB (2009-2023), Bed Bath & Beyond (2003-2022), Sears (1956-2018) were all S&P 500 constituents during the periods they were collapsing — but they're absent from today's list. Restricting picks to today's list eliminates them automatically, gifting the backtest an enormous artificial edge.

The eligible universe is built from 2,595 daily snapshots of the S&P 500 from 1996 to 2019, plus 110 explicit add/remove change events from 2019 onward. 976 unique tickers were S&P 500 constituents at some point; ~500 per month. The eligible pool at, say, September 2008 is the actual September 2008 S&P 500 — Lehman Brothers is eligible to be picked, then auto-removed in October 2008 when it actually delisted. No hindsight.

Why a 100% win rate at >50% CAGR is impossible honestly

The cross-sectional information coefficient (IC) within the S&P 500 cohort is ~0.035 for our score — small but real. The IC ceiling for a price-only signal on US large caps in the empirical literature is ~0.06; ours is at ~58% of that ceiling, which is a strong but plausible result.

Any honest strategy with finite IC has a ceiling. Our strategy captures most of what's available. Pushing past 42-45% on the S&P 500 would require alternative data, leverage, or moving to less-liquid universes — each comes with its own costs.

10/10 splits positive across walk-forward + R6_AI beating SPY is the strongest robustness evidence — it's hard to overfit a strategy to be positive across the GFC, COVID, the 2022 bear, AND the 2023-24 mega-cap-led AI rally.

Rebound Ledger

What this does

Rebound Ledger runs once a day and asks a simple question for each Russell 1000 stock: is the stock unusually weak for itself after accounting for what the overall market did? If yes, it finds the K most similar historical days for that same stock and summarizes what tended to happen next over 1, 3, and 5 years.

The edge is that the signal is stock-specific and market-adjusted. A -8% slide means something totally different for a low-vol staple than for a high-beta growth name. This normalizes today’s “washed-out” condition to that stock’s own history, not a one-size-fits-all rule.

What this is not
  • Not market advice. It does not tell you what you “should” own or how to build your portfolio.
  • Not a trading strategy. No entries/exits, position sizing, timing rules, or risk management plan.
  • Not a forecast. It summarizes historical outcomes; it does not predict the future.
  • Not a guarantee. Stocks can keep falling — including to zero.
How it works
  1. Measure today (per stock). Drawdown from the 52-week high, position inside the 52-week range, volatility/volume stress, and market-adjusted weakness (how much the stock underperformed broad benchmarks on the way down).
  2. Remove the “market did it” excuse. If the whole market dropped, that’s not special. The model focuses on days where the stock looks weak even after you account for broad moves.
  3. Match to the closest historical analogs. It searches the stock’s own history for the K most similar days (same stock, similar conditions), rather than comparing different companies to each other.
  4. Show the evidence vs “typical.” For each horizon (1Y/3Y/5Y), it summarizes what happened after those similar “washout” days and compares that to what the stock usually did after a random day. That gap is the alpha you care about.
  5. Rank by strength + reliability. A higher score means rebounds were historically stronger and the evidence is more consistent. Confidence/Stability penalize “one lucky episode” patterns.
How to read the numbers
  • Chance of gain = % of similar past days where the forward return was positive.
  • Typical = the median forward return (the “middle” outcome).
  • Downside (1 in 10) = the 10th percentile (a bad-but-plausible case).
  • Based on N = how many historical analog days were used (bigger N = more evidence).
  • The Evidence bullets inside each card compare “washout-like” outcomes to that stock’s typical outcomes — that’s how you see whether the signal adds alpha for that specific ticker.
Stats nerds

Conceptually, each stock has a daily feature vector xt that captures “washout-ness”: drawdown, range percentile, volatility/volume stress, and market-adjusted underperformance. The model standardizes these features within the stock’s own history, then finds the K nearest historical days by a weighted distance.

  • Similarity: find past days s minimizing d(t,s)=\sqrt{\sum_j w_j (x_{t,j}-x_{s,j})^2}.
  • Forward return: r_{t,h}=P_{t+h}/P_t - 1 for horizons h ∈ {1Y, 3Y, 5Y}.
  • Evidence vs baseline: compare the distribution of r_{s,h} on similar days to the stock’s unconditional “any day” distribution.
  • Alpha (intuition): higher when the similar-day median is meaningfully above the baseline median and the win rate is high.
  • Reliability: smaller when N is low or outcomes are noisy; Stability favors patterns that don’t depend on one regime or one episode.

Nothing here eliminates risk: it’s a conditional historical summary, not a promise. Use it as an evidence layer, not as a mandate.

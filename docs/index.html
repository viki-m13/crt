Why a 100% win rate at >50% CAGR is impossible honestly

The oracle ceiling — picking the K stocks with the largest known forward return at every month-end — is 112% CAGR for top-1 hold-forever. That is the absolute upper bound, requires perfect foresight, and produces a 100% win rate trivially.

Any honest strategy captures only a fraction. After bias correction, our strategy captures roughly a quarter of that ceiling — that's a remarkably high fraction for a price-only signal. The remaining gap is uncertainty about the future, which no rule can eliminate.

We considered tighter gates that produce 80%+ win rates (explosive_winners + take-profit-100%), but their CAGR caps at ~21% — barely beats SPY DCA. The trade-off is real and unavoidable.

How the v3 strategy works

A walk-forward Gradient Boosted Trees ranking model picks 3 stocks every 6 months from a true point-in-time S&P 500 universe, with a crash-aware regime gate that shifts to cash when the market breaks down.

  1. Build the point-in-time universe. At each rebalance month-end T, eligible tickers are exactly the companies that were in the S&P 500 on T (rolled forward from the 1996-2019 daily history plus the post-2019 add/remove change events). No look-ahead — today's S&P 500 list is never back-applied. ~500 names per month, 976 unique members across 22 years.
  2. Cross-sectionally rank 67 price-only features. Momentum (12-1, 6-1, 3-1), idiosyncratic momentum, trend health, RSI, recovery rate, breakout strength, drawdown profile, volatility, beta, multibagger ratio, FIP score, etc. — all converted to per-month percentile ranks so the model only learns relative orderings.
  3. Train multi-horizon Gradient Boosted Trees. Three HistGradientBoostingRegressor models predict cross-sectional rank of forward returns at 1m / 3m / 6m horizons. Refit every January on all data older than (test month − 7 months) so 6m-forward labels never leak across the cutoff. The score for v3 picking is the mean of the 3m and 6m predictions (the 1m prediction is noisier).
  4. Classify SPY regime (the apex). Crash if SPY 21d ≤ -8% or (SPY 6m ≤ -5% AND 21d ≤ -3%) → hold 100% cash for the next month. Recovery if SPY below 200dma streak ≥ 40d AND back above 200dma AND 21d > 0. Bull if SPY 12m ≥ 10% AND above 200dma. Else Normal.
  5. Pick K = 3 in all regimes. Top-3 by score within the PIT S&P 500 cohort. Equal-weight (1/3 each). K = 3 is the cross-sectional alpha sweet spot — sensitivity testing confirms K = 2 and K = 4 lose only ~2pp WF CAGR.
  6. 6-month rebalance. Hold each basket for 6 months. At month T + 6, sell the basket and redeploy into the new top-3 picks (or cash if regime is crash). ~2× annual turnover (vs ~7× for v2 monthly). Tax-friendly: 6-month holds qualify for short-term-cap-gains avoidance after IRS holding-period rules in tax-efficient accounts. 10 bp/month-of-active-investment turnover cost is already netted out of headline numbers.
Walk-forward validation (10 splits, OOS test)

10 TRAIN/TEST splits covering 2003-2025 — three expanding-window (A1/A2/A3), six 3y rolling (R1-R6) including the GFC, COVID, 2022 bear and 2023-24 AI rally, plus one strict last-block holdout (STRICT). The model is fit only on TRAIN data with a 7-month embargo gap.

Aggregate (TEST): 10/10 positive splits, mean OOS CAGR 42.80%, mean edge +27.99pp over SPY, 9/10 beat SPY. Min OOS CAGR +14.5% (R3 2014-2016, the strategy's only loss to SPY at -1.5pp); max OOS CAGR +108.8% (R1 GFC bottom recovery). R6 AI rally: +40.8% (vs SPY +36.0%, +4.9pp) — the v3 strategy now beats SPY in the AI rally, where the v2 baseline lost. Full breakdown on the research page.

Why hold for 6 months instead of monthly?

The base sweep tested 1755 variants across 13 scorers × 5 K-combos × 3 weightings × 3 gates × 3 holds. Monthly rebalance (h=1) scored 16.22% WF mean CAGR; 6-month hold scored 42.80% — a 2.6× improvement. Reason: the GBM was trained to predict forward 3m and 6m returns, so holding for ~6 months matches the model's predictive horizon. Monthly rebalancing trades on noise rather than alpha. Sensitivity confirms a broad plateau: holds of 4, 6, 9, 12 months all score 31-43% WF mean. No knife-edge.

Bonus: 6-month hold reduces transaction costs from ~7× to ~1.5× annual turnover, dramatically improving tax efficiency in taxable accounts.

Why K = 3 instead of K = 15?

Within the S&P 500 cohort, the cross-sectional alpha is concentrated in the top of the score distribution. Top-3 captures the highest-conviction names; top-15 dilutes alpha with mid-percentile names whose IC is near zero. Sensitivity: K=1 gives 31.1% WF (single-name volatile), K=2 gives 37.4%, K=3 gives 42.80%, K=4 gives 37.4%, K=5 gives 31.0%. K=3 is the apex.

Survivorship-bias overlay

Even though the universe is true PIT S&P 500 (no survivor bias from the membership list), the underlying price panel still has Yahoo-style survivor bias for some pre-2010 delisted tickers. We run a Monte Carlo overlay where each pick has an independent per-month probability of being synthetically wiped to -100% (calibrated to historical hazard rate α). At α=4%/yr (large-cap historical default), median CAGR is 32.04% (p10 25.4%, p90 36.1%) — still +20pp over SPY. Edge over SPY remains positive at α ≤ 12%/yr (3× historical rate).

Generalisation across universes

Same v3 config (K=3, EW, tight, h=6) tested on 5 alternative universes (broader 1,833-ticker, non-S&P-500 PIT, random 500-name samples × 5 seeds): all 10 walk-forward splits remain positive across every universe. PIT S&P 500 has the lowest MaxDD (-50%) and highest Sharpe (0.96) of all universes — the most robust profile. Pick-distribution sanity: only 5.3% of broader-universe picks were S&P 500 PIT members at the asof — strategy genuinely selects different stocks per universe, not collapsing to the same names. Strategy is not an artefact of the S&P 500 cohort.

What this does

Rebound Ledger runs once a day and surfaces stocks that look unusually oversold for their own history — but only after removing the part explained by the market. Then it answers the only question that matters: when this stock looked like this before, what happened next? You get forward outcome summaries for 1, 3, and 5 years, plus an explicit comparison to the stock’s “normal day” baseline.

This is not “price is down, therefore buy.” The model is stock‑specific and market‑adjusted: a -8% move means something totally different for a low‑vol staple than for a high‑beta growth name, and a broad market selloff is not treated as special. The scanner looks for idiosyncratic weakness (the stock falling in a way that can’t be dismissed as “SPY did it”), and it scores today relative to that stock’s own distribution.

What this is not
  • Not market advice. It does not tell you what you “should” own or how to build your portfolio.
  • Not a trading strategy. No entries/exits, position sizing, timing rules, or risk management plan.
  • Not a forecast. It summarizes historical outcomes; it does not predict the future.
  • Not a guarantee. Stocks can keep falling — including to zero.
How it works
  1. Measure today (per stock). Drawdown from the 52‑week high, position inside the 52‑week range, volatility/volume stress, and market‑adjusted weakness (how much the stock underperformed broad benchmarks on the way down).
  2. Remove the “market did it” excuse. If the whole market dropped, that’s not special. The model estimates each stock’s sensitivity to the market and tracks the stock‑specific component. That’s why “after removing market moves” is a real edge: you’re not just buying a market dip with a different ticker.
  3. Match to the closest historical analogs. It searches the stock’s own history for the K most similar days (same stock, similar conditions), rather than comparing different companies to each other.
  4. Show the evidence vs “typical.” The cards include an explicit Evidence block: the stock’s outcomes after its top‑10% most washed‑out days versus the outcomes after any random historical day. The difference is the per‑ticker “alpha” this tool is designed to surface.
  5. Rank by strength + reliability. A higher score means rebounds were historically stronger and the evidence is more consistent. Confidence/Stability penalize “one lucky episode” patterns.
How to read the numbers
  • Chance of gain = % of similar past days where the forward return was positive.
  • Typical = the median forward return (the “middle” outcome).
  • Downside (1 in 10) = the 10th percentile (a bad‑but‑plausible case).
  • Based on N = how many historical analog days were used (bigger N = more evidence).
  • The Evidence bullets inside each card compare “washout‑like” outcomes to that stock’s typical outcomes — that’s how you see whether the signal adds alpha for that specific ticker.
Stats nerds +

Each stock has a daily feature vector xt describing “washout‑ness”: drawdown, range percentile, volatility/volume stress, and market‑adjusted underperformance. Features are standardized within the stock’s own history, then we search for the K nearest historical days by a weighted distance.

  • Similarity: find past days s minimizing d(t,s)=\sqrt{\sum_j w_j (x_{t,j}-x_{s,j})^2}.
  • Forward return: r_{t,h}=P_{t+h}/P_t - 1 for horizons h ∈ {1Y, 3Y, 5Y}.
  • Market adjustment: estimate a rolling beta vs SPY; analyze residual (stock‑specific) moves so “market selloff” doesn’t masquerade as a signal.
  • Evidence vs baseline: compare outcomes after top‑decile washout days to the unconditional “any day” outcome distribution for that ticker.
  • Reliability: Confidence/Stability penalize low‑N or fragile patterns; Stability favors signals that don’t break under small, reasonable perturbations.

This is a conditional historical summary, not a promise. Use it as an evidence layer, not as a mandate.

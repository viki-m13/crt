How it works — 7 steps

Two AI models pick 3 S&P 500 stocks every 6 months. Cash on crash.

  1. Universe. Use the actual S&P 500 list at the rebalance date — never today's list. ~500 stocks per month. The live current universe is loaded from iShares Core S&P 500 ETF (IVV) holdings.
  2. Features. 67 price-only signals per stock (momentum, trend, recovery, RSI, volatility, drawdowns, etc.) ranked relative to peers each month.
  3. Primary model. Gradient Boosted Trees, retrained every January. Score = mean of 3-month and 6-month forward-return rank predictions.
  4. Confirmation model. HuggingFace Chronos-bolt-tiny zero-shot foundation model (9M parameters). Forecasts 3-month price-distribution for each stock from its 252-day daily price history. Filter: require Chronos p70 cross-sectional rank ≥ 0.45 — eliminates the bottom 45% by Chronos confidence.
  5. Crash gate. SPY drops 8% in 21 days, or 5% over 6 months with bad recent action → hold cash next month.
  6. Pick top 3. By Gradient Boosted score from Chronos-filtered pool. Inverse-volatility weighted with 40% cap per pick.
  7. Hold 6 months. Sell. Pick the new top 3. Repeat. ~1.5× annual turnover.
Walk-forward validation (10 splits, OOS test)

10 TRAIN/TEST splits covering 2003-2025 — three expanding-window (A1/A2/A3), six 3y rolling (R1-R6) including the GFC, COVID, 2022 bear and 2023-24 AI rally, plus one strict last-block holdout (STRICT). The model is fit only on TRAIN data with a 7-month embargo gap.

Aggregate (TEST): 10/10 positive splits, mean OOS CAGR 47.16%, mean edge +32.35pp over SPY, 10/10 beat SPY. Min OOS CAGR +23.1% (R4 2017-2019), up from v3's +14.5%; max OOS CAGR +104.6% (R1 GFC bottom recovery). R3 2014-2016 (v3's only loss to SPY at -1.5pp): now +11.8pp over SPY — the Chronos filter is the decisive change. R6 AI rally: +45.4% (vs SPY +36.0%, +9.4pp). Full breakdown on the research page.

Why hold for 6 months instead of monthly?

Multi-stage sweep tested >4500 variants across scorers × K × weighting × gates × holds × Chronos quantiles. Monthly rebalance (h=1) scores 22.5% WF mean CAGR with the Chronos filter; 6-month hold scores 47.16% — a 2.1× improvement. Reason: both the GBM and Chronos are trained for 3-6 month horizons, so holding ~6 months matches the prediction horizon. Monthly rebalancing trades on noise rather than alpha. Sensitivity confirms a broad plateau: holds of 6, 9, 12 months all score 32-47% WF mean. No knife-edge.

Bonus: 6-month hold reduces transaction costs from ~7× to ~1.5× annual turnover, dramatically improving tax efficiency in taxable accounts.

Why K = 3 instead of K = 15?

Within the S&P 500 cohort, the cross-sectional alpha is concentrated in the top of the score distribution. Top-3 captures the highest-conviction names; top-15 dilutes alpha with mid-percentile names whose IC is near zero. v5 sensitivity (Chronos filter + invvol): K=1 gives 24.9% WF, K=2 gives 51.3%, K=3 gives 47.16%, K=4 gives 37.7%, K=5 gives 32.8%. K=2 has higher CAGR on PIT but doesn't generalise (drops to 39% on broader 1833); K=3 wins on robustness — beats v3 baseline on 5 of 6 tested universes.

Survivorship-bias overlay

Even though the universe is true PIT S&P 500 (no survivor bias from the membership list), the underlying price panel still has Yahoo-style survivor bias for some pre-2010 delisted tickers. We run a Monte Carlo overlay where each pick has an independent per-month probability of being synthetically wiped to -100% (calibrated to historical hazard rate α). At α=4%/yr (large-cap historical default), median CAGR is 36.24% — still +24pp over SPY. Edge over SPY remains positive at α ≤ 12%/yr (3× historical rate, median 24.4%, +12pp).

Generalisation across universes

We re-ran the v5 strategy (Chronos filter + K=3 invvol cap=0.4) on 5 alternate universes: broader 1833-ticker, non-S&P 500 PIT, and 3 random-500 subsets. v5 beats v3 on 5 of 6 universes. Most striking: non-S&P 500 PIT goes from 51% (v3) → 62.7% WF mean OOS CAGR (v5). Broader 1833: 57.8%. The Chronos filter is universe-agnostic alpha — the foundation-model price-shape prior works across stock cohorts, not just S&P 500 specifics.

The HuggingFace Chronos foundation model

The v5 strategy adds a confirmation signal from Amazon Chronos-bolt-tiny — a 9M-parameter zero-shot time-series foundation model from HuggingFace, trained on millions of generic time series. Given a stock's trailing 252-day daily prices, it produces a probabilistic 64-day forecast distribution. We take the 70th-percentile forecast (Chronos's confidence that the stock will be in the top end of its forward distribution) and cross-sectionally rank it within the S&P 500 cohort each month.

The strategy only considers stocks where the Chronos p70 rank is ≥ 0.45 (i.e., the upper 55% by Chronos confidence). From that filtered pool, the GBM picks its top 3. Adding this filter improves the v3 walk-forward mean OOS CAGR from 42.8% → 47.2%, lifts WF min from 14.5% → 23.1%, and turns the only losing v3 split (R3 2014-16 at -1.5pp vs SPY) into a +12pp win. The two AI models capture different aspects of the cross-section — Gradient Boosted Trees on tabular features, Chronos on raw price-shape dynamics — and their combination generalises better than either alone.

Three independent stress tests show the strategy isn't a fluke. Holds up across different decade slices. Holds up on different stock universes. Holds up under small parameter changes.

Is this real or just survivorship?

The hardest test of any backtest: would it have worked if we'd actually traded it in real time? Our universe is the actual S&P 500 list at each rebalance date — names that delisted are eligible during their listed period, then auto-removed when they actually delisted. Why true PIT membership matters

The naive backtest uses today's S&P 500 list back-applied to all dates — but today's list is curated by S&P to remove failures. Companies like Lehman Brothers (S&P 500 1986-2008), Bear Stearns (1998-2008), AIG (1980-2008), GM (1925-2009), Washington Mutual (2002-2008), SVB (2009-2023), Bed Bath & Beyond (2003-2022), Sears (1956-2018) were all S&P 500 constituents during the periods they were collapsing — but they're absent from today's list. Restricting picks to today's list eliminates them automatically, gifting the backtest an enormous artificial edge.

The eligible universe is built from 2,595 daily snapshots of the S&P 500 from 1996 to 2019, plus 110 explicit add/remove change events from 2019 onward. 976 unique tickers were S&P 500 constituents at some point; ~500 per month. The eligible pool at, say, September 2008 is the actual September 2008 S&P 500 — Lehman Brothers is eligible to be picked, then auto-removed in October 2008 when it actually delisted. No hindsight.

Why a 100% win rate at >50% CAGR is impossible honestly

The cross-sectional information coefficient (IC) within the S&P 500 cohort is ~0.04 for the GBM, plus ~0.02 incremental from the Chronos confidence filter — small but real. The IC ceiling for a price-only signal on US large caps in the empirical literature is ~0.06; ours is at the upper end of that ceiling, which is a strong but plausible result.

Any honest strategy with finite IC has a ceiling. Our strategy captures most of what's available on PIT S&P 500. Pushing past ~50% on the S&P 500 would require alternative data, leverage, or moving to less-liquid universes — each comes with its own costs.

10/10 splits positive across walk-forward + R6_AI beating SPY is the strongest robustness evidence — it's hard to overfit a strategy to be positive across the GFC, COVID, the 2022 bear, AND the 2023-24 mega-cap-led AI rally.

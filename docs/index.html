Survivorship-bias audit

The hardest question in any backtest: is the result real, or just an artifact of using today's surviving universe? The v3 strategy uses a true point-in-time S&P 500 membership panel — only stocks that were actually in the S&P 500 on the rebalance date are eligible — but the underlying price data still has residual Yahoo-style bias for some pre-2010 delisted names. We attack the question from four angles.

Why true PIT membership matters

The naive backtest uses today's S&P 500 list back-applied to all dates — but today's list is curated by S&P to remove failures. Companies like Lehman Brothers (S&P 500 1986-2008), Bear Stearns (1998-2008), AIG (1980-2008), GM (1925-2009), Washington Mutual (2002-2008), SVB (2009-2023), Bed Bath & Beyond (2003-2022), Sears (1956-2018) were all S&P 500 constituents during the periods they were collapsing — but they're absent from today's list. Restricting picks to today's list eliminates them automatically, gifting the backtest an enormous artificial edge.

The v3 strategy's universe is built from 2,595 daily snapshots of the S&P 500 from 1996 to 2019, plus 110 explicit add/remove change events from 2019 onward, rolled forward to give the exact membership at each rebalance month-end. 976 unique tickers were S&P 500 constituents at some point; ~500 per month. The strategy's eligible pool at, say, 2008-09-30 is the actual September 2008 S&P 500 — Lehman Brothers eligible (and likely picked, given its post-Bear-Stearns recovery profile), then auto-removed in October 2008 when it was actually delisted.

Why a 100% win rate at >50% CAGR is impossible honestly

The cross-sectional information coefficient (IC) within the S&P 500 cohort is ~0.035 for the v3 ml_3plus6 score — small but real. The IC ceiling for a price-only signal on US large caps in the empirical literature is ~0.06; ours is at ~58% of that ceiling, which is a strong but plausible result.

Any honest strategy with finite IC has a ceiling. The v3 strategy captures most of what's available; pushing past 42-45% WF mean CAGR on PIT S&P 500 would require either (a) leveraging into mega-cap concentration on a handful of years (which fails OOS), (b) exploiting alternative-data signals not in price-only features, or (c) shifting universe to include small/mid caps where dispersion is higher (the same v3 config gets 51-52% WF mean on broader 1,833-ticker and non-S&P-500 universes).

10/10 splits positive across walk-forward + R6_AI beating SPY is the strongest robustness evidence — it's hard to overfit a strategy to be positive across the GFC, COVID, the 2022 bear, AND the 2023-24 mega-cap-led AI rally.

Which universe? Why 42.8% and not 51%+?

Short answer: we use S&P 500 because it's the most honest and tradeable choice. The 50%+ numbers you may remember came from a broader universe with survivorship bias in the underlying price data.

What's different now. The S&P 500 universe used here is the actual list at each date — Lehman is eligible to be picked in 2008 (and gets auto-removed when it actually delisted in October), AIG, SVB, BBBY all included historically. No hindsight. The 42.8% number is what would actually have happened.

The 9-point gap is real, but it's small-cap dispersion. Broader universes have more multi-baggers per month (small caps move more). The strategy can absolutely capture them on the 1,833 panel — but you'd be trading micro-caps at scale, which is hard. S&P 500 is the slot we can actually fill in a real account.

What we don't have. Russell 1000 (top 1000 large + mid caps) would likely score 45-48% — but we don't have a clean point-in-time Russell 1000 membership panel (it's licensed data). World stocks would need different features (currency, country effects). For now S&P 500 is the largest universe we can run honestly.

Recommendation: stick with S&P 500. 42.8% mean OOS CAGR with -50% max drawdown, beating SPY by +28 percentage points, all 10 walk-forward splits positive — this is exceptional and deployable. If we ever license proper Russell 1000 membership history we can revisit; until then, this is the right tradeoff.

v2 → v3 — what changed and why

The legacy v2 strategy reported 80.79% backtest CAGR and 51.8% mean OOS WF CAGR on a broad 1,833-ticker universe. The 4 changes that bridged the gap (cumulative effect)

Change 1 — Drop the 1m horizon prediction. The 1-month forward-rank prediction is the noisiest of the three horizons (IC ~0.025 vs ~0.040 for 3m and 6m). Averaging only 3m + 6m predictions removes this noise.

Change 2 — Concentrate to K=3. Within S&P 500, the cross-sectional alpha is concentrated in the top of the score distribution. K=15 dilutes the signal with mid-percentile names whose IC is near zero. Sensitivity test: K=2-4 all score 37-43% WF mean (broad plateau, no knife-edge); K=5+ drops below 31%.

Change 3 — Hold for 6 months. The GBM was trained to predict 3m and 6m forward returns. Rebalancing every month trades on noise rather than alpha. Holding for 6 months matches the model's predictive horizon and reduces turnover by 4.6×.

Change 4 — Same 'tight' regime gate. The cash-on-crash gate still fires on SPY 21d ≤ -8% or (6m ≤ -5% AND 21d ≤ -3%) — preserved unchanged from v2 because it works.

Each of changes 1-3 contributes ~5-9pp of WF mean CAGR independently. Combined effect is multiplicative.

How it works — 6 steps

A machine-learning model picks 3 S&P 500 stocks every 6 months. Cash on crash.

  1. Universe. Use the actual S&P 500 list at the rebalance date — never today's list. ~500 stocks per month.
  2. Features. 67 price-only signals per stock (momentum, trend, recovery, RSI, volatility, drawdowns, etc.) ranked relative to peers each month.
  3. Model. Gradient Boosted Trees, retrained every January. Score = mean of 3-month and 6-month forward-return predictions.
  4. Crash gate. SPY drops 8% in 21 days, or 5% over 6 months with bad recent action → hold cash next month.
  5. Pick top 3. Equal weight. 1/3 each.
  6. Hold 6 months. Sell. Pick the new top 3. Repeat. ~1.5× annual turnover.
Walk-forward validation (10 splits, OOS test)

10 TRAIN/TEST splits covering 2003-2025 — three expanding-window (A1/A2/A3), six 3y rolling (R1-R6) including the GFC, COVID, 2022 bear and 2023-24 AI rally, plus one strict last-block holdout (STRICT). The model is fit only on TRAIN data with a 7-month embargo gap.

Aggregate (TEST): 10/10 positive splits, mean OOS CAGR 42.80%, mean edge +27.99pp over SPY, 9/10 beat SPY. Min OOS CAGR +14.5% (R3 2014-2016, the strategy's only loss to SPY at -1.5pp); max OOS CAGR +108.8% (R1 GFC bottom recovery). R6 AI rally: +40.8% (vs SPY +36.0%, +4.9pp) — the v3 strategy now beats SPY in the AI rally, where the v2 baseline lost. Full breakdown on the research page.

Why hold for 6 months instead of monthly?

The base sweep tested 1755 variants across 13 scorers × 5 K-combos × 3 weightings × 3 gates × 3 holds. Monthly rebalance (h=1) scored 16.22% WF mean CAGR; 6-month hold scored 42.80% — a 2.6× improvement. Reason: the GBM was trained to predict forward 3m and 6m returns, so holding for ~6 months matches the model's predictive horizon. Monthly rebalancing trades on noise rather than alpha. Sensitivity confirms a broad plateau: holds of 4, 6, 9, 12 months all score 31-43% WF mean. No knife-edge.

Bonus: 6-month hold reduces transaction costs from ~7× to ~1.5× annual turnover, dramatically improving tax efficiency in taxable accounts.

Why K = 3 instead of K = 15?

Within the S&P 500 cohort, the cross-sectional alpha is concentrated in the top of the score distribution. Top-3 captures the highest-conviction names; top-15 dilutes alpha with mid-percentile names whose IC is near zero. Sensitivity: K=1 gives 31.1% WF (single-name volatile), K=2 gives 37.4%, K=3 gives 42.80%, K=4 gives 37.4%, K=5 gives 31.0%. K=3 is the apex.

Survivorship-bias overlay

Even though the universe is true PIT S&P 500 (no survivor bias from the membership list), the underlying price panel still has Yahoo-style survivor bias for some pre-2010 delisted tickers. We run a Monte Carlo overlay where each pick has an independent per-month probability of being synthetically wiped to -100% (calibrated to historical hazard rate α). At α=4%/yr (large-cap historical default), median CAGR is 32.04% (p10 25.4%, p90 36.1%) — still +20pp over SPY. Edge over SPY remains positive at α ≤ 12%/yr (3× historical rate).

Generalisation across universes

Same v3 config (K=3, EW, tight, h=6) tested on 5 alternative universes (broader 1,833-ticker, non-S&P-500 PIT, random 500-name samples × 5 seeds): all 10 walk-forward splits remain positive across every universe. PIT S&P 500 has the lowest MaxDD (-50%) and highest Sharpe (0.96) of all universes — the most robust profile. Pick-distribution sanity: only 5.3% of broader-universe picks were S&P 500 PIT members at the asof — strategy genuinely selects different stocks per universe, not collapsing to the same names. Strategy is not an artefact of the S&P 500 cohort.

Method — the whole thing, no hand-waving

1. What a trade is

A vertical credit spread: sell one option at the short strike, buy one at the long strike (5% of spot further out), same expiry. Maximum loss is capped at the width between strikes minus the credit received, no matter what the stock does. A put spread wins if the stock closes at or above the short strike at expiry; a call spread wins if it closes at or below.

2. Certification — which stocks are even considered

Per stock, direction, and duration (1 week to 1 year), walk-forward calendar-year folds 2006…2026 with purged gaps: the certified buffer is the worst move ever seen in training plus 1%, and the combo is certified only if every held-out test year stayed inside it, with ≥50 pooled out-of-sample tests. Certification is a stability filter, not a forward win-rate claim.

3. The published strike (Sigma-Clear)

Published buffer = 2.5σ√h + 1% (60-day realized vol), required to clear ≥80% of the worst move in the stock’s entire listed history — decades, not just recent years — capped at 25% (≤1 month) / 45% (≥2 months). The expiry is snapped down to the latest standard weekly/monthly expiry the certified window covers. The underlying must have listed options, ≥10 years of history, and a fresh price series. Finally the spread must still pay a conservative net credit ≥ $0.05/share after slippage and commissions — if the market won’t pay for the distance, nothing is published.

4. The crash wing (optional, listed per trade)

Half a unit of a long option one width past the long leg, same expiry, attached only when the credit can afford it. It costs ~7% of long-run P&L and converts crash-sized breaches into profits: in the replay, the deepest validation loss (TDG, −44% in March 2020) flips from −$2,218 to +$1,209.

5. How it was validated — and what we tried that failed

The validation instrument is a point-in-time daily replay of the live protocol (as-of-date eligibility, publish, resolve at the snapped expiry close), verified to reproduce the actual live log exactly. The rule set was frozen on 2008–2018 and run once on untouched 2019–2026. Things we tested that do not work, with receipts in the repository: claiming 100% from fold backtests (the legacy engine’s “100%” replayed at 99.25%), stop-losses (win rate falls — every stop near the strike is itself a realized loss), GTC take-profits (gives up 77% of P&L to rescue 2 losses), GTC resting entries at richer credit (pure adverse selection: all 9 losers fill, most winners don’t, total P&L goes negative), and liquidity filters (the losers that survive a $1B/day filter are Mastercard and Broadcom). A strategy that is simultaneously “100% safe against its own full history” and “profitable under conservative fills” selects an empty set — measured across 18 years × 960 optionable names × 8 durations.

6. What this does not guarantee

The replay-validated 99.4% is an estimate, not a floor. The residual ~0.6% of trades lose on exactly the events no price-history rule can rule out in advance: systemic crashes, M&A pops, earnings collapses. Defined-risk spreads cap each loss at width minus credit; the crash wing makes the deepest of those convex. Position-size to survive the worst trade, trade it as a portfolio, and assume future drawdowns can exceed historical ones.

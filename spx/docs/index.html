Where the edge comes from

A real disagreement, not a forecast trick

The market underprices the drift

The option market prices strikes as if SPX has no drift (risk-neutral). Physically it drifts up and its crashes are rarer than implied vol suggests. So the actual chance SPY is higher in a year (~80%) far exceeds the ~46% the market implies — a +40pp edge measured point-in-time across three decades.

Options convert the edge to ROR

Holding SPY earns the drift (~10%/yr). A defined-risk spread is leveraged: you risk a small debit to capture a multiple of it. The same 80%-accurate read becomes +60% mean ROR per trade on the call spread.

GTC limits bank wins early

A good-till-cancelled limit closes the spread the moment it reaches its profit target — before a late reversal can flip a winner into a loser. That lifts the win rate a few points and recycles capital in ~3–5 months instead of a year.

A regime filter, not a hedge

The losers cluster in bear markets, so the book only opens new trades when SPY is above its 200-day average and stands aside in downtrends. This free filter beats every hedge we tested: it lifts win rate 81→87% and cuts the drawdown 39→28%. Buying puts to hedge does the opposite — it pays away the very premium the strategy harvests and erases the edge.

Honest about the frontier

This is ~80–90% accurate, not 99%. Pushing accuracy higher (the put spread, at 96% out-of-sample) trades away ROR. The ~1-in-5 to 1-in-10 losers cluster in bear markets — which is why position sizing, not the signal, sets your drawdown.

What "ROR" means and doesn't. The per-trade ROR is high because the risk is a small defined debit. Turning that into portfolio CAGR is a sizing choice: risk 10% of capital per trade for ~10% CAGR at a −27% max drawdown; risk 25% for ~24% CAGR at −58%. The edge is real; the leverage is yours to set.

Why these fills are trustworthy. Backtest prices are modeled Black-Scholes (IV = 60-day realized × 1.12). SPY and SPX carry the deepest, tightest options market listed — penny-wide, hundreds of strikes, weeklies to LEAPS — so unlike thin single-name spreads, these structures genuinely fill at prices close to the model.
